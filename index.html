Horror film genre: history, characteristics and subgenres

The horror film genre has existed as a genre since the mid-1930s. In its beginnings, this genre was greatly influenced by the Gothic literature of authors such as Bram Stoker, Allan Poe or Mary Shelley. Basing itself on this literature for its creation was a success, since cinema is the perfect medium to capture all the characteristics of Gothic literature. As for the genre in the cinema, obviously one of the characteristics of the horror genre is to scare the viewer . Although it seems obvious from the point of view we have today, the simple fact of going to see a movie at the cinema today acquires a totally different concept from almost a century ago.

In the mid-30s of the last century, going to the movies to be scared was a mission that not everyone was capable of doing. There were even times when movie theaters were left half empty when spectators fled in terror. Despite this, the first horror films were a complete success among the public , their popular acceptance being instantaneous and the foundations of the genre being established very soon. Due to the success of these horror films, some studios (especially Universal ) realized the commercial success of these films and it was here that the history of horror films began .

Normally, the plots of horror movies revolve around the appearance of an "evil" force that terrifies the protagonists and they must fight against adversity to save themselves. Over the decades, the genre has evolved, emerging numerous subgenres of horror movies such as:

  • B-Series Horror
  • Science fiction horror
  • Slasher
  • Zombie movies
  • Gore movies

Horror movie history

The antecedents of horror movies come from, as we have said in the introduction, the horror novel. In addition to these novels that were born in the second half of the 18th century, previously there was already a tradition of scary tales passed down from generation to generation. A highly developed tradition in rural cultures around the world.

If we stick to the strictly cinematographic, it can be said that the first film in history was also a horror film. We are talking about the film by the Lumière brothers, L'arrivée d'un train à La Ciotat (The arrival of the train at Ciotat station) from 1896 . This film caused great terror in the spectators, the cinema was a new invention and when they saw the train that was approaching, the public believed that it was going to leave the screen to run over them. It can be said that, then, cinema has been linked to terror since its inception. Since, even if it is a minimum, we give the cinema more credibility than the books.

Influences of German Expressionism

In 1923, Universal Pictures began producing films with horror and gothic elements. The protagonist of almost all of these films was Lon Chaney , who soon became a benchmark in horror movies thanks to his avant-garde makeup and his talent. The three dark icons of his filmography are The Hunchback of Notre Dame (1923) , The Phantom of the Opera (1925) and London After Midnight (1927) , perhaps the most legendary and influential lost film of all time.

The first film in the series was The Hunchback of Notre Dame. In this film Lon Chaney surprised the world with his performance and characterization as the hunchback Quasimodo (you can see the image below). It would be an adaptation of the Victor Hugo novel that Alice Guy already adapted in 1905. Two years later, in 1925, Chaney would star in The Phantom of the Opera . Which is considered one of the best adaptations of Gastón Leroux's novel and also one of the best interpretations of Chaney.

In the 1930s, Universal Pictures continued to produce films based on gothic horror literature. And it laid the foundation for what would become the horror film genre . In this decade, the studio produced several films featuring the most iconic monsters in movie history: Dracula, Frankenstein, The Mummy, The Invisible Man, and The Wolf Man.

  • Dracula (1931)
  • Frankenstein (1931)
  • The Bride of Frankenstein (1935)
  • The Mummy (1932)
  • The Invisible Man (1933)

Cult horror from the 40s and 50s that you should know about

Terror and the cult is something that has gone hand in hand since the very existence of the genre , and it is not only the hegemony of the genre of the seventies and eighties (which is when the label itself takes more prominence). You just have to take a look at the first milestones of horror in the cinema, which would achieve that classification for all the influence they had and the indisputable legacy they generated for filmmakers and subsequent currents.

Already in the early twenties, ' The Cabinet of Doctor Caligari ' and ' Nosferatu ' are not only the two great totems of terror that few dare to question , but also become the object of deserved worship that, almost a century after its existence remains impassive to the passage of time. A decade later , and almost always associated with what would come to be understood as series B, the other genre films attached to this category would come from filmmakers like Tod Browning or James Whale , who with ' The monster stop ' and ' Doctor Frankenstein' set a chair in terms of the conception of terror on screen is concerned.

  • Creature from the Black Lagoon (1954)

From the 70s to the present

The 70s was the decade where the great blockbusters of the genre were created in the modern era and many of these films have come down to us as horror film references today. Movies like The Texas Chainsaw Massacre (1974), The Exorcist (1973) or The Prophecy (1976) give an account of this. Also in the 1970s, the works of horror author Stephen King began to be adapted for the big screen, beginning with Brian De Palma's adaptation of Carrie (1976) , King's first published novel, in which the two protagonists female ( Sissy Spacek and Piper Laurie) earned Oscar nominations. His third published novel would be the basis for Stanley Kubrick to create The Shining (1980) . At that time the film was a critical and public failure but over time it has become one of the most iconic films in the horror genre .

In Spain, films of the genre were also made, and Who can kill a child? (1976) by Chicho Ibáñez Serrador is an example. Also noteworthy in Italy is Dario Argento with Suspiria (1977) and in Japan, Hausu (1977) , a kind of most original and surreal terrifying comedy that horror movies have produced. Already entered the eighties, the genre would be filled with increasingly violent and bloody films where a psychopath persecuted the protagonists ( Slasher subgenre that we will see later). These types of films will continue to be made for decades to come. Films like Friday the 13th (1980),A Nightmare on Elm Street (1984), or Hellraiser (1987)
